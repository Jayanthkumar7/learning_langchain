================================================================================
                          THE COMPLETE GUIDE TO CRICKET
================================================================================

A comprehensive guide covering the basics, equipment, rules, and famous players
of cricket — the world's second most popular sport, followed by 2.5 billion fans
across more than 100 countries.

================================================================================
SECTION 1: WHAT IS CRICKET?
================================================================================

Cricket is a bat-and-ball game played between two teams of eleven players on a
large oval-shaped field. At the centre of the field is the "pitch," a rectangular
strip of ground approximately 22 yards (20.12 metres) long, where most of the
action takes place.

Each team takes turns to bat (score runs) and field (take wickets). The team with
the most runs at the end of the game wins.

Matches range from short Twenty20 (T20) games lasting about 3 hours, to One Day
Internationals (ODIs) lasting around 8 hours, to Test matches that can last up to
5 days.

The Laws of Cricket were written by the Marylebone Cricket Club (MCC) in 1788 and
there are 42 official Laws in total. The International Cricket Council (ICC) governs
cricket at the international level.

SECTION 2: EQUIPMENT USED IN CRICKET
================================================================================

1. CRICKET BAT
   - Made of willow wood (English willow or Kashmir willow)
   - Flat on one side (the hitting face), curved (ridge) on the other
   - Maximum length: 38 inches (96.5 cm)
   - Maximum width: 4.25 inches (10.8 cm)
   - The blade is made of wood; the handle is wrapped in rubber grip
   - English willow bats are considered premium quality; used by professionals
   - Kashmir willow bats are more affordable; good for beginners

2. CRICKET BALL
   - A hard, leather-covered ball stitched with a prominent seam
   - Red ball: used in Test matches and first-class cricket
   - White ball: used in ODIs and T20 matches
   - Pink ball: used in day-night Test matches
   - Weight: between 155.9 g and 163 g
   - Circumference: between 22.4 cm and 22.9 cm
   - The seam and condition of the ball (shine on one side) play a crucial role
     in a bowler's ability to swing the ball through the air

3. STUMPS AND BAILS (THE WICKET)
   - Three vertical wooden stumps hammered into the ground at each end of the pitch
   - Two small wooden pieces (bails) rest on top of the stumps
   - Width of the wicket: 9 inches (22.86 cm)
   - Height of the stumps: 28 inches (71.1 cm)
   - A batsman is "out bowled" when the ball hits the stumps and dislodges the bails

4. PROTECTIVE GEAR (for batsmen)
   - Helmet: hard outer shell with a metal grille to protect the face
   - Batting gloves: padded gloves to protect hands and fingers
   - Batting pads (leg guards): foam and plastic guards strapped to the legs
   - Thigh pad: protects the upper thigh from fast deliveries
   - Inner thigh guard: protects the inner thigh
   - Chest guard: protects the chest and arm from short-pitched deliveries
   - Abdominal guard (box): protects the groin area
   - Arm guard: optional; protects the forearm

5. WICKET-KEEPER'S GEAR
   - Wicket-keeping gloves: large, webbed gloves for catching the ball
   - Wicket-keeping pads: shorter and lighter than batting pads
   - Wicket-keepers also wear helmet, chest guard, and abdominal guard

6. FIELDING EQUIPMENT
   - Fielders do not use protective equipment (except when in close positions)
   - Fielding caps and sunglasses are commonly used

7. THE PITCH
   - 22 yards (20.12 m) long, 10 feet (3.05 m) wide
   - Prepared clay surface, typically rolled and flattened
   - Has a "popping crease" (batting crease) and "bowling crease" at each end
   - The pitch changes over time — it can crack and deteriorate, which helps
     spin bowlers later in a match

8. THE FIELD
   - An oval or circular grassy field with no fixed size
   - Typically 100–160 yards across
   - The "boundary" is marked by a rope around the edge of the field
   - A ball reaching the boundary scores 4 runs; a ball hit over the boundary
     without bouncing scores 6 runs

9. CRICKET SHOES
   - Spiked shoes (cricket spikes) for grip on grass and clay surfaces
   - Rubber-soled shoes for indoor or artificial surface cricket

10. SCOREBOARD AND TECHNOLOGY
    - Traditional manual scoreboard (numbers on boards)
    - Modern electronic scoreboards in international venues
    - Snickometer (Snicko): detects faint edges using sound
    - Hawk-Eye: ball-tracking technology for LBW reviews
    - Hot Spot: infrared imaging to detect ball-bat contact
    - Ultra Edge: combination of Snicko and real-time audio waveform

SECTION 3: FORMATS OF CRICKET
================================================================================

1. TEST CRICKET
   - The oldest and most prestigious format
   - Each team bats twice (two innings each)
   - No limit on overs; a match can last up to 5 days
   - Teams wear traditional white clothing
   - A red ball is used

2. ONE DAY INTERNATIONAL (ODI)
   - Each team bats for a maximum of 50 overs (300 balls)
   - Usually completed in one day (~8 hours)
   - Coloured clothing and a white ball are used
   - Powerplay rules restrict fielders in the first 10 overs

3. TWENTY20 (T20)
   - The shortest and most explosive format
   - Each team bats for a maximum of 20 overs (120 balls)
   - Completed in about 3 hours
   - Popularised by tournaments like the IPL (Indian Premier League),
     Big Bash League (BBL), and PSL (Pakistan Super League)

4. THE HUNDRED
   - A newer format introduced in England (2021)
   - Each team faces 100 balls, not overs
   - Bowlers can bowl 5 or 10 balls in a row

SECTION 4: BASIC RULES OF CRICKET
================================================================================

--- THE TOSS ---
Before the match, the two captains meet at the pitch. One captain tosses a coin;
the other calls heads or tails. The winner decides whether to bat or field first.

--- TEAMS AND INNINGS ---
- Each team has 11 players
- Teams take turns batting and fielding
- In Test cricket: each team bats twice (two innings)
- In ODIs and T20s: each team bats once

--- HOW RUNS ARE SCORED ---
- Running between the wickets: both batsmen run to opposite ends = 1 run each crossing
- Batting pairs can run 1, 2, or 3 runs on a single hit
- Boundary (ball rolls to the rope): 4 runs, batsmen don't need to run
- Six (ball cleared the rope without bouncing): 6 runs
- Extras: runs given to the batting team without the bat touching the ball
  * Wide: ball bowled too wide for the batsman to hit
  * No-ball: bowler overstepped the crease, or bowled illegally
  * Bye: ball missed bat and keeper; batsmen run
  * Leg bye: ball hit the batsman's pads, not the bat; batsmen run

--- HOW A BATSMAN IS DISMISSED (WAYS OF GETTING OUT) ---
There are 10 ways a batsman can be dismissed:

1. BOWLED — The ball hits the stumps and dislodges the bails
2. CAUGHT — The ball is hit by the bat and caught by a fielder before it bounces
3. LBW (Leg Before Wicket) — The ball hits the batsman's pad (not the bat) and
   would have gone on to hit the stumps
4. RUN OUT — A fielder hits the stumps while a batsman is outside the crease
   while running
5. STUMPED — The wicket-keeper removes the bails while the batsman is out of the
   crease and not attempting a run
6. HIT WICKET — The batsman accidentally hits the stumps with the bat or body
7. HANDLED THE BALL — The batsman deliberately touches the ball with the hand
   not holding the bat (now covered under "Obstructing the Field")
8. OBSTRUCTING THE FIELD — A batsman deliberately obstructs a fielder
9. HIT THE BALL TWICE — The batsman hits the ball twice intentionally
10. TIMED OUT — A new batsman takes more than 3 minutes to arrive at the crease

--- BOWLING RULES ---
- A "over" consists of 6 legal deliveries bowled from the same end
- After each over, the bowling switches to the opposite end
- The same bowler cannot bowl consecutive overs
- A bowler must not bowl more than 10 overs in ODIs and 4 in T20s (per match)
- No-balls are penalised: the batting team gets an extra delivery
- In Test cricket, there is no limit on how many overs a bowler can bowl

--- POWERPLAY ---
In limited-overs cricket (ODI and T20), Powerplays restrict the number of fielders
allowed outside the inner fielding circle:
- ODI: Mandatory Powerplay = first 10 overs; maximum 2 fielders outside the circle
- T20: Mandatory Powerplay = first 6 overs; maximum 2 fielders outside the circle

--- LBW (LEG BEFORE WICKET) RULE (IN DETAIL) ---
A batsman is out LBW if:
- The ball was not a wide
- The ball pitched in line or on the off side
- The ball hit the batsman's pad (not the bat) first
- The ball would have gone on to hit the stumps (umpire's judgment or DRS)

If the ball pitches outside leg stump, the batsman CANNOT be given out LBW.

--- DRS (DECISION REVIEW SYSTEM) ---
Each team gets a limited number of reviews per innings to challenge umpire decisions.
Technology used: Hawk-Eye (ball tracking), Ultra Edge (sound/contact), Hot Spot.
A review is retained if the on-field decision is overturned.

--- STOP-CLOCK RULE (2024) ---
Introduced in men's ODIs and T20Is from June 2024:
- The bowling team must begin a new over within 60 seconds of the previous one ending
- After two warnings, every further breach results in 5 penalty runs to the batting team

--- FIELDING RESTRICTIONS ---
- A maximum of 5 fielders may be on the leg side at any time
- In ODIs: no more than 5 fielders outside the 30-yard fielding circle after Powerplay
- In T20s: no more than 5 fielders outside during non-Powerplay overs

--- BALL TAMPERING ---
Deliberately altering the condition of the ball (scratching, using saliva with
foreign substances, etc.) is illegal and results in a 5-run penalty. The saliva
ban (using saliva to shine the ball) was made permanent post-COVID.

--- WINNING THE MATCH ---
- The team with the most runs at the end of all innings wins
- In a limited-overs match that is interrupted by rain: DLS (Duckworth-Lewis-Stern)
  method recalculates targets
- If a limited-overs match ends in a tie: a Super Over (1-over-per-side shootout)
  is used to determine the winner in most tournaments

SECTION 5: FIELDING POSITIONS IN CRICKET
================================================================================

Fielding positions are named based on where they are placed relative to the batsman.
Key positions include:

ON THE OFF SIDE (right side for a right-handed batsman):
- Slip (1st, 2nd, 3rd) — close to the wicket-keeper; catches edges
- Gully — wider than slips
- Point — square on the off side
- Cover point — between point and mid-off
- Mid-off — close to the bowler on the off side

ON THE LEG SIDE:
- Fine leg — behind the batsman on the leg side
- Square leg — level with the batsman on the leg side
- Mid-on — close to the bowler on the leg side
- Mid-wicket — between mid-on and square leg
- Long-on — deep on the leg side near the boundary

BEHIND THE WICKET:
- Wicket-keeper — directly behind the stumps
- Long stop — very rarely used; behind the wicket-keeper near the boundary

SECTION 6: BOWLING TYPES AND TECHNIQUES
================================================================================

PACE BOWLING (Fast Bowling)
- Fast bowlers aim to bowl at high speeds (130–150+ km/h) to beat the batsman
- Types of deliveries:
  * Outswinger: ball swings away from the right-handed batsman in the air
  * Inswinger: ball swings into the right-handed batsman
  * Reverse swing: old ball swings in the opposite direction to conventional swing
  * Yorker: full-pitched delivery aimed at the batsman's feet
  * Bouncer: short-pitched delivery that rises sharply toward the batsman's head
  * Slower ball: disguised delivery bowled at reduced pace to deceive the batsman

SPIN BOWLING
- Spinners bowl slower, relying on turning the ball off the pitch
  * Off-spin (finger spin): ball turns from off side to leg side for right-handers
  * Leg-spin (wrist spin): ball turns from leg side to off side; more varied
  * Doosra: an off-spinner's delivery that turns the other way
  * Googly (wrong'un): a leg-spinner's delivery that turns the other way
  * Carrom ball: flicked with the middle finger; unpredictable turn
  * Flipper: a leg-spinner's quicker, lower delivery that skids through

MEDIUM-PACE BOWLING
- Bowled at medium speeds (110–130 km/h)
- Relies on seam movement (ball landing on the seam and deviating off the pitch)
- Types: seam-up, cutters (off-cutter, leg-cutter)

SECTION 10: CRICKET GLOSSARY
================================================================================

Crease         — Line on the pitch marking the safe zone for batsmen
Duck           — Dismissed for 0 runs (Golden duck = on the first ball)
Hat-trick      — Bowler takes 3 wickets in 3 consecutive deliveries
Maiden over    — An over in which no runs are scored
Century (100)  — A batsman scores 100 or more runs in one innings
Half-century   — A batsman scores 50 runs or more
Follow-on      — When a team forced to bat again immediately in a Test
Full toss      — Ball that reaches the batsman without bouncing
Half volley    — Ball that bounces just short of the batsman's feet
Beamer         — Illegal full-toss directed at the head; dangerous delivery
Mankad         — Bowler runs out the non-striker while in delivery stride
Duckworth-Lewis— Mathematical method to recalculate rain-affected match targets
Chinaman       — A left-arm wrist-spinner's stock delivery (turns like an off-break)
Doosra         — Off-spinner's delivery that turns the opposite way
Flipper        — Leg-spinner's faster, skidding delivery
Super over     — 1-over per side mini-match to decide a tied limited-overs game
Powerplay      — Mandatory fielding restrictions at the start of an innings
Economy rate   — Average runs conceded per over by a bowler
Strike rate    — Runs scored per 100 balls (batting) or balls per wicket (bowling)
